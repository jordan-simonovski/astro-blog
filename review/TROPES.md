# AI Writing Tropes to Avoid

Source: tropes.fyi by ossama.is. Mirrored here so the skill never depends on a
live fetch. Re-sync periodically; the upstream list changes (this copy includes
the "Serves As" Dodge and Invented Concept Labels, added after the original
March 2026 version).

---

## Word Choice

### "Quietly" and Other Magic Adverbs

Overuse of "quietly" and similar adverbs to convey subtle importance or understated power. Also includes: "deeply", "fundamentally", "remarkably", "arguably".
Examples to avoid: "quietly orchestrating workflows, decisions, and interactions"; "a quiet intelligence behind it".

### "Delve" and Friends

Overused AI vocabulary: "delve", "certainly", "utilize", "leverage" (as a verb), "robust", "streamline", "harness".
Examples to avoid: "Let's delve into the details..."; "We certainly need to leverage these robust frameworks...".

### "Tapestry" and "Landscape"

Ornate or grandiose nouns where simpler words would do. Other offenders: "paradigm", "synergy", "ecosystem", "framework".
Examples to avoid: "The rich tapestry of human experience..."; "the ever-evolving landscape of technology...".

### The "Serves As" Dodge

Replacing simple "is" or "are" with pompous alternatives: "serves as", "stands as", "marks", "represents".
Examples to avoid: "The building serves as a reminder of the city's heritage."; "The station marks a pivotal moment...".

## Sentence Structure

### Negative Parallelism

The "It's not X, it's Y" pattern. The single most common AI tell. One per piece can be effective; ten is an insult. Includes the causal variant "not because X, but because Y", the dismissal "X, not Y", and the cross-sentence reframe "The question isn't X. The question is Y."
Examples to avoid: "It's not bold. It's backwards."; "Half the bugs you chase aren't in your code. They're in your head.".

### "Not X. Not Y. Just Z."

The dramatic countdown. Negating two or more things before revealing the point.
Examples to avoid: "Not a bug. Not a feature. A fundamental design flaw.".

### "The X? A Y."

Self-posed rhetorical question answered immediately for dramatic effect.
Examples to avoid: "The result? Devastating."; "The worst part? Nobody saw it coming.".

### Anaphora Abuse

Repeating the same sentence opening in quick succession.
Examples to avoid: "They assume that users will pay... They assume that developers will build...".

### Tricolon Abuse

Overuse of the rule-of-three, often extended to four or five. One is elegant; three back-to-back is a tell.
Examples to avoid: "Products impress people; platforms empower them. Products solve problems; platforms create worlds...".

### "It's Worth Noting"

Filler transitions that signal nothing. Also: "It bears mentioning", "Importantly", "Interestingly", "Notably".
Examples to avoid: "It's worth noting that this approach has limitations.".

### Superficial Analyses

Tacking a present-participle phrase onto a sentence to inject shallow analysis. "highlighting its importance", "reflecting broader trends".
Examples to avoid: "contributing to the region's rich cultural heritage".

### False Ranges

"From X to Y" where X and Y aren't on any real scale.
Examples to avoid: "From innovation to implementation to cultural transformation.".

## Paragraph Structure

### Short Punchy Fragments

Very short sentences or fragments as standalone paragraphs for manufactured emphasis.
Examples to avoid: "He published this. Openly. In a book. As a priest.".

### Listicle in a Trench Coat

Numbered points dressed up as prose: "The first... The second... The third...".
Examples to avoid: "The first wall is the absence of... The second wall is the lack of...".

## Tone

### "Here's the Kicker"

False-suspense transitions before an unremarkable point. Also: "Here's the thing", "Here's where it gets interesting", "Here's what most people miss".
Examples to avoid: "Here's the kicker."; "Here's the thing about AI adoption.".

### "Think of It As..."

The patronizing analogy. "Think of it as...", "It's like a...".
Examples to avoid: "Think of it like a highway system for data.".

### "Imagine a World Where..."

The AI invitation to futurism.
Examples to avoid: "Imagine a world where every tool you use has a quiet intelligence behind it...".

### False Vulnerability

Performative self-awareness. Polished, risk-free admissions.
Examples to avoid: "And yes, I'm openly in love with the platform model"; "This is not a rant; it's a diagnosis".

### "The Truth Is Simple"

Asserting something is obvious instead of proving it. Includes "but none of them is the real story. The real story is...".
Examples to avoid: "History is unambiguous on this point".

### Grandiose Stakes Inflation

Inflating every argument to world-historical significance.
Examples to avoid: "This will fundamentally reshape how we think about everything.".

### "Let's Break This Down"

Pedagogical hand-holding voice. Also: "Let's unpack this", "Let's explore", "Let's dive in".
Examples to avoid: "Let's break this down step by step.".

### Vague Attributions

Unnamed authorities: "experts", "observers", "industry reports". Inflating source counts.
Examples to avoid: "Experts argue that this approach has significant drawbacks.".

### Invented Concept Labels

Compound labels that sound analytical but aren't grounded: problem-noun (paradox, trap, creep, divide, vacuum, inversion) appended to a domain word.
Examples to avoid: "the supervision paradox"; "the acceleration trap"; "workload creep".

## Formatting

### Em-Dash Addiction

Compulsive em dashes for pauses, asides, pivots. 2-3 per piece is human; 20+ is AI.
Examples to avoid: "The problem -- and this is the part nobody talks about -- is systemic.".

### Bold-First Bullets

Every list item starting with a bolded phrase.
Examples to avoid: "**Security**: Environment-based configuration..."; "**Performance**: Lazy loading...".

### Unicode Decoration

Unicode arrows, smart/curly quotes, characters not easily typed.
Examples to avoid: "Input → Processing → Output"; curly quotes instead of straight quotes.

---

## Composition

### Fractal Summaries

Summaries at every level. "What I'm going to tell you; what I'm telling you; what I just told you."
Examples to avoid: "In this section, we'll explore... [3000 words later] ...as we've seen in this section.".

### The Dead Metaphor

One metaphor beaten into the ground 5-10 times.
Examples to avoid: "The ecosystem needs ecosystems to build ecosystem value.".

### Historical Analogy Stacking

Rapid-fire listing of companies or tech revolutions to build false authority. Common in technical writing.
Examples to avoid: "Apple didn't build Uber. Facebook didn't build Spotify. Stripe didn't build Shopify...".

### One-Point Dilution

One argument restated ten ways across thousands of words.

### Content Duplication

Sections or paragraphs repeated verbatim within the same piece.

### The Signposted Conclusion

Announcing the conclusion: "In conclusion", "To sum up", "In summary".
Examples to avoid: "In conclusion, the future of AI depends on...".

### "Despite Its Challenges..."

Acknowledging problems only to dismiss them. "Despite its [positive], [subject] faces challenges..." then "Despite these challenges, [optimistic conclusion].".

---

Remember: any pattern used once may be fine. The problem is when multiple tropes
appear together or one trope is used repeatedly. Write like a human: varied,
imperfect, specific.
